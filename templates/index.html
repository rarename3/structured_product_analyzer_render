Structured Product Types We Analyze

Primary Objective: Preserve invested capital while providing some participation in market upside
Construction: Majority invested in zero-coupon bond (for capital protection) + small portion in options (for upside)
Typical Investor: Conservative investors, retirees, risk-averse individuals seeking modest growth
Key Risks: Inflation risk, opportunity cost, issuer credit risk, limited upside participation
Best For: Sideways or moderately bullish markets where capital preservation is priority

1. Fixed Coupon PPNs
Provide regular fixed interest payments throughout the term plus return of principal at maturity. The coupon rate is typically lower than traditional bonds because of the embedded option component.
Structure: Bond + at-the-money call option on underlying asset
Payout: Fixed coupon payments + 100% principal + potential upside if underlying appreciates
Example: 5-year PPN with 3% annual coupon linked to S&P 500
Risk Profile: Lowest risk among PPNs, predictable income stream
Cost Structure: Lower option cost allows for higher bond allocation

2. Participation PPNs
Offer direct exposure to the performance of an underlying asset with full capital protection. Participation rate determines how much of the upside is captured.
Structure: Zero-coupon bond + out-of-the-money call option(s)
Payout: 100% principal + (Underlying Return × Participation Rate)
Key Feature: Participation rate typically 50–100%; may have caps on maximum return
Example: PPN with 80% participation in NASDAQ gains, capped at 25% total return
Trade-off: Higher participation = lower bond allocation = higher risk

3. Autocallable PPNs
Automatically redeem early if the underlying asset reaches predetermined trigger levels, providing potential for early return of principal plus premium.
Structure: Bond + autocall feature with barrier options
Observation: Periodic observation dates (quarterly/semi-annually)
Trigger: If underlying ≥ autocall level on observation date, product redeems
Example: 3-year autocallable PPN with 110% trigger level and 8% coupon if called
Advantage: Potential for higher yield if called early
Disadvantage: Reinvestment risk if called in low-rate environment

1. Full Capital Guarantee
100% of principal is guaranteed regardless of underlying performance. The guarantee is typically provided by the issuer or through third-party insurance.
Mechanism: Entire bond component used for protection; options funded from issuer's capital
Cost: Higher cost structure reduces potential returns
Use Case: Absolute capital preservation requirements (e.g., endowment funds)
Regulation: Often requires special licensing for issuers
Market: Popular in Europe and Asia, less common in US

2. Partial Capital Guarantee
Only a portion of principal is guaranteed (typically 80–95%), allowing for higher potential returns through larger option allocation.
Trade-off: Lower protection allows for higher participation rates or lower costs
Risk Profile: First loss buffer of 5–20% before capital erosion begins
Example: 90% capital guarantee with 120% participation in gold price appreciation
Investor Type: Moderate risk tolerance, seeking balance between protection and growth
Pricing: More cost-effective than full guarantee products

Primary Objective: Generate above-market income by selling options and collecting premiums
Construction: Bond + short option positions (writing calls/puts to earn income)
Typical Investor: Income-focused investors willing to accept moderate capital risk for higher yields
Key Risks: Capital loss if barriers breached, complexity risk, issuer risk
Best For: Sideways or range-bound markets where volatility can be harvested

1. Single-Underlying Reverse Convertibles
Pay enhanced coupons but convert into shares of the underlying stock if it falls below a predetermined barrier at maturity.
Structure: Bond + short put option on single stock
Conversion: If stock price < barrier at maturity, investor receives shares instead of cash
Yield Source: Enhanced coupon = risk-free rate + option premium
Risk: Exposed to significant decline in single stock
Barrier Types: European (at maturity), American (anytime), Parisian (consecutive days)
Example: 6-month reverse convertible on AAPL with 70% barrier and 12% annualized coupon

2. Multi-Underlying Reverse Convertibles
Similar to single-underlying but linked to basket of stocks, typically with "worst-of" payoff where the poorest performer determines the outcome.
Structure: Bond + worst-of put options on basket
Diversification: Reduces single-stock risk but introduces correlation risk
Payout: If ANY stock in basket < barrier, conversion occurs based on worst performer
Example: Reverse convertible linked to basket of 5 tech stocks with 70% barrier
Correlation Risk: High correlation increases risk of conversion
Pricing: More complex due to basket option pricing

1. Phoenix Autocallables
Pay coupons only when the underlying asset is above a predetermined barrier on observation dates. No coupon payments if barrier is breached.
Structure: Bond + digital call options with memory feature
Coupon Condition: Coupon paid only if underlying ≥ coupon barrier on observation date
Risk: Potential for zero coupon payments during product life
Yield: Higher potential coupons to compensate for conditional payments
Observation: Typically monthly or quarterly observation dates
Example: 3-year Phoenix autocallable with 10% coupon if S&P 500 ≥ 80% of initial level

2. Memory Autocallables
Similar to Phoenix but with "memory" feature where missed coupons are accumulated and paid later if conditions are met.
Memory Feature: Unpaid coupons accumulate and are paid when underlying recovers above barrier
Advantage: Reduces path dependency compared to standard Phoenix
Complexity: More complex pricing and higher cost
Investor Appeal: Attractive for volatile markets where recoveries are expected
Pricing Impact: Memory feature reduces coupon rate compared to standard Phoenix

1. Digital Range Accruals
Pay a fixed coupon for each day the underlying asset stays within a predetermined range. No coupon for days outside the range.
Structure: Series of daily digital options that pay if underlying within range
Accrual: Coupon = (Days in range / Total days) × Fixed rate
Ideal Market: Low volatility, range-bound markets
Risk: Significant reduction in coupons if underlying breaks out of range
Range Types: Constant range, widening range, step-up range
Example: Range accrual with 10% coupon for days EUR/USD between 1.05–1.15

2. Variable Coupon Range Accruals
Similar to digital but with variable coupon rates that depend on how far within the range the underlying is, or with different rates for different range segments.
Structure: Digital options with varying payoffs based on position within range
Variations: Can include step-up rates, tiered ranges, or participation rates
Complexity: More difficult to model and value
Investor Benefit: Higher potential coupons if underlying stays in "sweet spot"
Example: 5% coupon in outer range, 10% in middle range, 15% in inner range

Primary Objective: Provide amplified exposure to underlying assets or direct market participation
Construction: Primarily options-based with little or no bond component
Typical Investor: Sophisticated investors, traders, those with high risk tolerance
Key Risks: Amplified losses, time decay (theta), volatility risk, gap risk
Best For: Strong directional views with defined time horizons

1. With Capital Protection
Provide participation in both upside and downside movements but with caps on gains and protection from losses.
Structure: Zero-cost collar (long call + short put) with different strikes
Upside: Participate in gains up to cap (e.g., 20%)
Downside: Participate in losses with floor (e.g., -10% maximum loss)
Example: Twin-win with 20% gain cap and 10% loss floor
Cost: Typically zero premium structure
Use Case: Moderate volatility markets with defined ranges

2. Without Capital Protection
Higher participation rates in exchange for full capital risk. Essentially a geared version of direct investment.
Structure: Leveraged long position using options or futures
Participation: Can offer 150–200% of underlying return
Risk: Full capital at risk with amplified losses
Example: 150% participation in oil price with no downside protection
Investor Type: High conviction on direction, short time horizon
Pricing: Lower cost than protected versions

1. Bull Certificates
Provide leveraged long exposure (typically 2x or 3x) to an underlying asset. Daily rebalancing means they track daily returns, not long-term returns.
Mechanism: Use swaps or futures to create synthetic leveraged position
Rebalancing: Daily rebalancing causes decay in volatile sideways markets
Holding Period: Designed for short-term trading, not long-term investing
Example: 2x Bull Certificate on gold tracking 2x daily gold price moves
Volatility Drag: Significant negative effect in high volatility environments
Liquidity: Exchange-traded with good liquidity in major markets

2. Bear Certificates
Provide leveraged short exposure, profiting when the underlying asset declines. Inverse of bull certificates with similar characteristics.
Structure: Short position via swaps or futures with daily rebalancing
Use: Hedging or speculative bearish bets
Risk: Unlimited risk in theory (if underlying rises significantly)
Example: 3x Bear Certificate on technology sector ETF
Decay: Positive decay in sideways or up markets (works against holder)
Margin: No margin requirements for certificate holders

1. Dividend Adjusting Trackers
Track the performance of an underlying asset while adjusting for dividend payments, either by reinvesting dividends or paying them out.
Structure: Total return swap replicating underlying + dividends
Variants: Price return (ex-dividend) vs. total return (with dividends)
Tax: Different tax treatment than direct stock ownership
Example: S&P 500 tracker with quarterly dividend payments
Tracking Error: Minimal with efficient swap structures
Cost: Lower than actively managed products

2. Outperformance Certificates
Designed to outperform the underlying asset through option overlays or structured payoffs while maintaining similar risk profile.
Mechanism: Tracker + option strategy (e.g., covered call writing)
Outperformance: Achieved through yield enhancement or volatility capture
Cost: Higher fees for active management component
Example: Outperformance certificate with 110% participation and downside buffer
Risk: Capped upside potential in exchange for downside protection
Market Condition: Best in sideways or slightly bullish markets

Primary Objective: Access complex strategies, multiple asset classes, or hard-to-reach markets
Construction: Highly complex derivatives, multiple underlying assets, exotic options
Typical Investor: Institutional investors, hedge funds, ultra-high-net-worth individuals
Key Risks: Liquidity risk, model risk, correlation breakdown, tail risk, complexity risk
Best For: Portfolio diversification, specific thematic exposure, sophisticated strategies

1. Worst-of Basket Products
Performance determined by the worst-performing asset in a basket. Typically offer higher coupons but significant downside risk.
Structure: Bond + put option on worst performer in basket
Correlation: Most sensitive to correlation between basket components
Payout: Full payout only if ALL components above barriers
Risk: One bad performer can wipe out entire investment
Example: Worst-of basket of 10 emerging market currencies
Pricing Complexity: Requires correlation modeling and tail risk assessment

2. Best-of Basket Products
Performance determined by the best-performing asset in a basket. Lower risk than worst-of but with correspondingly lower potential returns.
Structure: Bond + call option on best performer in basket
Diversification: Benefits from diversification as only one component needs to perform well
Cost: More expensive than worst-of due to lower risk
Example: Best-of basket of green energy stocks
Investor Appeal: Thematic exposure with built-in selection mechanism
Correlation Impact: Low correlation improves product value

3. Rainbow Basket Products
Custom weighting or performance formula where different assets have different contributions to the final payout.
Structure: Complex option combinations with custom weights
Flexibility: Can overweight certain assets or create custom correlations
Complexity: Extremely difficult to value and risk-manage
Example: Rainbow basket with 50% tech, 30% healthcare, 20% energy
Use Case: Custom portfolio views and tactical allocations
Liquidity: Typically bespoke with limited secondary market

1. Multi-Asset Class Products
Combine exposure to multiple asset classes (equities, commodities, currencies, rates) in a single structured product.
Structure: Options on different asset classes with correlation plays
Objective: Portfolio diversification, inflation hedging, thematic plays
Example: Product linked to 40% equities + 30% commodities + 30% bonds
Correlation Management: Key determinant of performance and risk
Rebalancing: May include periodic rebalancing rules
Complexity: Multi-dimensional risk requiring sophisticated models

2. Structured Products with Insurance Features
Combine investment features with insurance elements like mortality protection, longevity risk transfer, or catastrophe bonds.
Structure: Investment component + insurance wrapper or derivatives
Examples: Variable annuities with guarantees, catastrophe-linked notes
Regulation: Often subject to both securities and insurance regulation
Example: Catastrophe bond paying enhanced coupons unless hurricane occurs
Risk Transfer: Transfers insurance risk to capital markets
Pricing: Requires actuarial modeling in addition to financial modeling

1. Volatility Products
Provide direct exposure to volatility indices (like VIX) or volatility itself as an asset class.
Structure: Futures/options on volatility indices or variance swaps
Contango: Suffer from contango in VIX futures, causing roll decay
Use: Portfolio hedging, speculative volatility plays
Example: VIX-linked note with 2x exposure to VIX futures
Risk: Extreme volatility spikes can cause dramatic losses
Hedging Use: Effective portfolio diversifier during market stress

2. Inflation-Linked Products
Returns linked to inflation indices (CPI) with floors, caps, or leveraged exposure.
Structure: Inflation swaps, options on inflation indices
Protection: Often include floors to protect against deflation
Example: CPI + 2% with 0% floor and 5% cap
Use Case: Retirement planning, real return objectives
Indexation Lag: CPI reporting lag can affect performance timing
Real Yield: Provides real (inflation-adjusted) returns

3. Commodity-Linked Products
Exposure to commodity prices with structured payoffs, often including features like rolling futures, backwardation plays, or seasonal patterns.
Structure: Commodity futures, options, or swaps with enhancements
Roll Yield: Can capture positive roll yield in backwardated markets
Example: Oil-linked note with Asian-style averaging and 20% downside buffer
Storage Costs: Embedded in futures pricing structure
Seasonality: Can exploit seasonal patterns in agricultural commodities
Diversification: Low correlation with traditional assets
