What Instruments Can We Assess?

Designed for investors who prioritize safeguarding their principal. These instruments allocate most of the notional to a fixed-income component while using a smaller portion for option-based upside exposure. They suit conservative portfolios, retirement savings, and endowment mandates where downside protection outweighs return maximization.

Fixed Coupon Variants

Deliver periodic fixed interest on top of full principal repayment at maturity. The coupon is funded partly by an embedded at-the-money call, meaning rates tend to be lower than comparable corporate bonds.

How it works: Zero-coupon bond combined with a call option on the reference asset
Return profile: Steady income stream plus any residual upside from the option leg
Typical deal: 5-year note paying 3% p.a. referenced to S&P 500
Who buys these: Income-oriented investors wanting bond-like safety with equity-linked potential
Participation Variants

Offer a percentage share in the underlying's gains while guaranteeing the original investment. The participation rate — typically 50-100% — reflects how much upside the investor captures.

How it works: Bond floor plus out-of-the-money call options
Return profile: Principal + (asset gain x participation rate), sometimes capped
Typical deal: 80% participation in NASDAQ gains, maximum return capped at 25%
Key trade-off: Higher participation requires accepting either a cap on gains or a thinner bond floor
Autocallable Variants

Include an early-redemption trigger: if the reference asset hits a predetermined level on an observation date, the product matures early and pays a premium above par.

How it works: Bond plus knock-in autocall barrier options
Observation schedule: Quarterly or semi-annual check dates
Typical deal: 3-year note, 110% trigger, pays 8% coupon if called early
Upside: Higher yield when called early | Downside: Reinvestment risk in a low-rate environment

100% Guarantee Structures

The issuer or a third-party insurer backs the full notional regardless of market outcomes. Common among European and Asian private-banking products, less prevalent in the US due to regulatory overhead.

Funding: The entire bond allocation goes to principal protection; option premium is absorbed by the issuer
Cost implication: Tighter return potential compared to partial-guarantee cousins
Typical buyer: Endowment funds and fiduciary accounts with zero-loss mandates
Partial Guarantee Structures (80-95%)

Guarantee only a fraction of the notional — typically 80% to 95%. The freed-up capital finances a larger option component, resulting in higher participation rates or richer coupons.

Risk buffer: Investor absorbs the first 5-20% of losses before the guarantee kicks in
Typical deal: 90% guarantee with 120% participation in gold prices
Who buys these: Balanced-mandate investors comfortable with a small loss cushion in exchange for better upside

Built for investors who want above-market yields and are prepared to accept conditional capital risk. The income typically comes from selling (writing) option positions — collecting premium in exchange for bearing tail-event losses. These instruments perform best when markets trade sideways or within a defined corridor.

Single-Reference Variants

Pay an above-market coupon in return for the risk that the investor receives shares instead of cash at maturity if the stock breaches a knock-in barrier.

How it works: Bond combined with a short put on a single equity
Income source: Risk-free rate plus the put-option premium collected
Barrier styles: European (checked at expiry), American (continuous), Parisian (consecutive-day breach)
Typical deal: 6-month note on AAPL, 70% barrier, 12% annualised coupon
Multi-Reference / Worst-of Variants

Linked to a basket of equities under a worst-of payoff rule — if any single name breaches its barrier, the conversion is based on the weakest performer.

How it works: Bond plus worst-of put options across the basket
Correlation dependency: Higher inter-stock correlation raises breach probability
Typical deal: 5-stock tech basket, 70% barriers, enhanced coupon vs. single-name version

Phoenix-Style (Conditional Coupon)

Coupons are paid only when the reference asset sits above a coupon barrier on each observation date. If the barrier is breached, no coupon accrues for that period.

How it works: Bond plus a series of digital call options
Income risk: Possible zero-income periods if the asset stays below the barrier
Typical deal: 3-year note, 10% conditional coupon when S&P 500 stays above 80% of strike
Memory-Style (Accumulated Coupon)

Missed coupons are banked and paid out in full once the asset recovers above the barrier — reducing the path-dependency problem of standard Phoenix notes.

Benefit: Recovery in the asset "unlocks" all previously missed payments
Trade-off: Memory feature lowers the headline coupon rate vs. a plain Phoenix
Best suited for: Volatile markets where mean-reversion is expected

Digital (Binary) Accruals

Earn a fixed daily coupon whenever the reference asset closes inside a predefined corridor. Days outside the corridor contribute nothing.

Income formula: (Days inside corridor / Total days) x Annual rate
Corridor styles: Static, widening, step-up over time
Typical deal: 10% rate for days EUR/USD stays between 1.05 and 1.15
Variable-Rate Accruals

Coupon rate changes depending on where inside the corridor the asset sits — paying more when it is near the centre and less near the edges.

Tiered example: 5% in outer zone, 10% in middle zone, 15% in centre zone
Complexity: Requires multi-layer digital-option modelling

Aimed at traders and sophisticated investors who hold strong market views and want amplified exposure. These instruments are heavily options-based with little or no bond floor, meaning gains — and losses — are magnified. They are best used with short time horizons and disciplined risk management.

With a Loss Floor

Participate in both rising and falling markets, but gains are capped and losses are floored (e.g., max -10%). Structured as a zero-premium collar.

Typical deal: 20% cap on gains, 10% maximum loss, zero net premium
Best suited for: Range-bound markets with moderate volatility
Without a Loss Floor

Higher participation (150-200% of asset return) but full principal is at risk. Essentially a leveraged directional position packaged as a note.

Typical deal: 150% participation in crude-oil moves, no downside buffer
Who buys these: Short-horizon traders with high conviction on direction

Bull (Long-Biased)

Deliver 2x or 3x the daily return of a reference asset using synthetic swap positions. Daily rebalancing makes them unsuitable for buy-and-hold — volatility drag erodes value in choppy markets.

Typical deal: 2x daily gold certificate, exchange-listed with intraday liquidity
Key risk: Compounding decay in high-volatility, sideways environments
Bear (Short-Biased)

Inverse certificates that profit when the reference asset falls. Carry the same daily-rebalancing mechanics — and risks — as their bull counterparts, plus theoretically unlimited loss if the asset rallies sharply.

Uses: Tactical hedging or speculative short bets without margin accounts
Typical deal: 3x inverse certificate on a technology-sector ETF

Dividend-Adjusted Trackers

Replicate the total return of a reference index — including reinvested dividends — via a swap wrapper. Tax treatment differs from direct equity ownership, which may be advantageous in certain jurisdictions.

Typical deal: S&P 500 total-return tracker with quarterly distributions
Tracking precision: Minimal drift when swap counterparties are well-capitalised
Outperformance Certificates

Layer an option overlay (e.g., covered-call writing) on top of a tracker to generate incremental alpha. The trade-off is capped upside in exchange for a yield boost or a downside buffer.

Typical deal: 110% participation with a built-in 10% downside cushion
Best market regime: Sideways or mildly bullish conditions

The most complex corner of the structured-product universe. These instruments span multiple asset classes, use exotic option payoffs, and often require bespoke pricing models. They are typically issued for institutional portfolios, hedge-fund mandates, or ultra-high-net-worth allocations where standard instruments cannot achieve the desired risk/return profile.

Worst-of Baskets

Final payout hinges on the weakest performer in a multi-asset basket. A single laggard can eliminate the entire return — or principal — making correlation modelling critical to fair pricing.

Typical deal: 10-currency EM basket, worst-of knock-in barrier
Key risk: Tail-event concentration in one component wipes out the whole position
Best-of Baskets

Payout is driven by the strongest performer. Only one asset needs to do well, so the product benefits from low correlation across components.

Typical deal: Best-of basket across clean-energy equities
Cost note: More expensive to structure than worst-of because the issuer bears more risk
Rainbow Baskets

Use custom weighting formulas — sometimes dynamically adjusted — so that different assets contribute unequally to the final payoff. Extremely difficult to replicate or hedge.

Typical deal: 50% tech / 30% healthcare / 20% energy, bespoke OTC issuance
Liquidity: Almost no secondary market; exit typically requires issuer buyback

Multi-Asset Class Notes

Bundle equities, commodities, rates, and FX into a single wrapper. Performance depends on inter-asset correlations, which can shift dramatically under stress.

Typical deal: 40% equity / 30% commodity / 30% fixed-income linked note
Modelling challenge: Multi-dimensional risk surface requires Monte Carlo simulation
Insurance-Linked Notes

Merge investment returns with insurance-risk transfer — for example, catastrophe bonds that pay enhanced coupons unless a defined natural disaster triggers a principal write-down.

Regulatory overlap: Falls under both securities and insurance regulation in most jurisdictions
Pricing: Requires actuarial loss models layered on top of financial derivatives pricing

Volatility-Linked Notes

Give direct exposure to implied or realised volatility — often via VIX futures or variance swaps. A powerful portfolio hedge during market sell-offs, but subject to severe contango-driven roll decay in calm markets.

Typical deal: 2x VIX-futures-linked note
Key risk: Extreme losses possible during volatility spikes and subsequent mean-reversion
Inflation-Indexed Notes

Returns are pegged to a consumer-price index, often with a deflation floor and an inflation cap. Useful for pension mandates and real-return-targeting portfolios.

Typical deal: CPI + 2%, floored at 0%, capped at 5%
Lag consideration: CPI data is reported with a delay, affecting mark-to-market timing
Commodity-Indexed Notes

Structured exposure to physical commodities — oil, metals, agriculture — without taking physical delivery. May incorporate Asian averaging, seasonal roll strategies, or downside buffers.

Typical deal: Oil-linked note with Asian averaging and a 20% loss cushion
Diversification value: Historically low correlation with equity and bond markets
